### FAQs to Include (using exact keywords)
1. **"What are the ACAT assessment levels?"**
   - Answer: ACAT assessment levels determine which Home Care Package you qualify for. Level 1 is for basic care needs, Level 2 for low-level needs, Level 3 for intermediate needs, and Level 4 for high-level care needs. The Aged Care Assessment Team evaluates your physical, medical, psychological, and social needs to recommend the appropriate level.

2. **"What is an ACAT assessment?"**
   - Answer: An ACAT assessment (Aged Care Assessment Team) is a free government assessment to determine your eligibility for aged care services including Home Care Packages and residential aged care. Assessors visit your home to evaluate your care needs, daily living abilities, and support requirements.

3. **"How do I prepare for an aged care assessment?"**
   - Answer: To prepare for an aged care assessment: have your Medicare card and ID ready, list your current medications, note your daily challenges and care needs, have a family member present if possible, and be honest about what you struggle with. The assessment typically takes 1-2 hours.

4. **"What is the difference between ACAT Level 2 and Level 3?"**
   - Answer: ACAT Level 2 (low-level care needs) provides approximately $18,622/year for people needing help with some daily tasks. Level 3 (intermediate care needs) provides $40,675/year for people needing more regular assistance with personal care, mobility, or health management.

5. **"How long does an ACAT assessment take?"**
   - Answer: An ACAT assessment typically takes 1-3 hours depending on your circumstances. Results usually arrive within 2-4 weeks. If you have urgent needs, request a priority assessment through My Aged Care on 1800 200 422.

### FAQs to Include (using exact keywords)
1. **"Can I work as an NDIS support worker without a qualification?"**
   - Answer: Yes, you can work as an NDIS support worker without a formal qualification in many cases. While qualifications like Certificate III in Individual Support are valued, they're not always mandatory. You'll need an NDIS Worker Screening Check, reliable transport, and the right personal qualities like empathy and reliability. Many employers provide on-the-job training.

2. **"What are the minimum requirements for NDIS support workers?"**
   - Answer: The minimum requirements for NDIS support workers are: 1) NDIS Worker Screening Check clearance, 2) Working rights in Australia, 3) Reliable transport (car preferred), 4) First Aid Certificate (often required), 5) Good communication skills, 6) Empathy and patience. Formal qualifications are preferred but not always essential for entry-level roles.

3. **"Do you need a qualification to be an NDIS support worker?"**
   - Answer: No, you don't always need a qualification to be an NDIS support worker. However, having a Certificate III or IV in Individual Support, Disability, or Aged Care will increase your job opportunities and pay rates. Some specialized roles (high-intensity supports, complex care) do require specific qualifications.

4. **"How do I become a support worker with no experience?"**
   - Answer: To become a support worker with no experience: 1) Get your NDIS Worker Screening Check, 2) Complete a First Aid course, 3) Consider volunteering to gain experience, 4) Apply for entry-level positions with providers who offer training, 5) Highlight transferable skills from other jobs, 6) Complete the free NDIS Worker Orientation Module online.

5. **"What disqualifies you from being a support worker?"**
   - Answer: You may be disqualified from support work if you: have certain criminal convictions (violent, sexual, or drug offenses), fail the NDIS Worker Screening Check, have a history of workplace misconduct in care settings, or are on the NDIS Worker Screening exclusion list. Minor offenses may not automatically exclude you.

### FAQs to Include (using exact keywords)
1. **"Where can I use my Companion Card NSW?"**
   - Answer: You can use your Companion Card NSW at participating venues including: cinemas (Event, Hoyts, Palace), theme parks (Luna Park, Taronga Zoo), sporting venues (SCG, Allianz Stadium), museums and galleries, public transport (free companion travel), concerts and festivals, and many restaurants and attractions. Look for the Companion Card logo or check the Companion Card website for the full directory.

2. **"What is a Companion Card NSW?"**
   - Answer: A Companion Card NSW is a free card for people with significant, permanent disability who need attendant care support to participate in community activities. When you visit participating venues, your companion (carer) gets free or discounted entry. The card is valid for 5 years and recognized across all Australian states and territories.

3. **"How do I apply for a Companion Card?"**
   - Answer: To apply for a Companion Card NSW: 1) Complete the application form online or in paper, 2) Have a health professional confirm your disability and care needs, 3) Provide proof of identity and residency, 4) Submit your application to Companion Card NSW. Processing takes 4-6 weeks. The card is free.

4. **"What is the difference between a Companion Card and a Carer Card NSW?"**
   - Answer: A Companion Card is for the person WITH disability - it allows their companion/carer free entry to venues. A Carer Card (like the Centrelink Carer Concession Card) is for the CARER and provides concessions on utilities and services. They serve different purposes and you may be eligible for both.

5. **"Who is eligible for a Companion Card?"**
   - Answer: You're eligible for a Companion Card if you: have a significant, permanent disability, are unable to participate in most community activities without attendant care, need this support for life (not temporary), and are an Australian resident. Age is not a barrier - children and adults can apply.

### FAQs to Include (using exact keywords)
1. **"How much NDIS SIL funding will I get?"**
   - Answer: NDIS SIL funding varies based on your individual support needs, typically ranging from $150,000 to $450,000+ per year. Factors include: your support ratio (1:1, 1:2, or 1:3), whether you need active overnight support or sleepover, your specific care requirements, and the location. The NDIA assesses your needs through a SIL quote process with your provider.

2. **"What is the cost of supported living?"**
   - Answer: Supported Independent Living (SIL) costs in Australia typically range from $150,000-$450,000+ per year through NDIS. Costs depend on: staff ratio (1:1 is most expensive), overnight support type (active night ~$85/hr vs sleepover ~$280/night), location, and complexity of care needs. You also pay reasonable rent and living costs separately.

3. **"What are the NDIS SIL rates for 2025?"**
   - Answer: NDIS SIL rates for 2025-26 include: Standard weekday support $70.88/hr, Evening/Saturday $78.23/hr, Sunday $100.17/hr, Public holiday $128.11/hr, Sleepover $282.78/night, Active overnight $85.22/hr. High-intensity supports are charged at higher rates. These are maximum price limits - actual costs depend on your SIL agreement.

4. **"How do I apply for SIL funding?"**
   - Answer: To get SIL funding: 1) Discuss SIL with your Support Coordinator or LAC, 2) Choose a SIL provider who will assess your needs, 3) The provider submits a SIL quote to the NDIA detailing your support requirements, 4) NDIA reviews and approves funding, 5) SIL is added to your NDIS plan. The process can take several months.

5. **"What is the difference between SIL and SDA?"**
   - Answer: SIL (Supported Independent Living) is funding for SUPPORT WORKERS to help you with daily living in shared housing. SDA (Specialist Disability Accommodation) is funding for the BUILDING itself - specialized housing with accessibility features. You can have SIL without SDA (in regular housing) or both together in purpose-built disability accommodation.

### FAQs to Include (using exact keywords)
1. **"What questions are asked in an NDIS functional capacity assessment?"**
   - Answer: NDIS functional capacity assessment questions cover: daily living activities (cooking, cleaning, personal care), mobility and physical function, communication abilities, cognitive function (memory, decision-making), social interaction, community participation, current support needs, goals and aspirations. The assessor will ask you to demonstrate or describe how you complete everyday tasks.

2. **"What is a functional capacity assessment example?"**
   - Answer: A functional capacity assessment example: The OT asks you to walk to the kitchen and make a cup of tea. They observe your mobility, balance, ability to follow steps, fine motor skills (opening containers), safety awareness, and problem-solving. They might also assess dressing, toileting, meal prep, and community access through observation and interview.

3. **"How do I prepare for an NDIS functional capacity assessment?"**
   - Answer: To prepare for an FCA: 1) Have a "bad day" mindset - describe your worst days, not best, 2) Bring supporting documents (medical reports, therapy notes), 3) List all your daily challenges, 4) Have a support person present, 5) Don't tidy up beforehand - show your real environment, 6) Be honest about what you struggle with, 7) Note any aids or equipment you use.

4. **"What does a functional capacity assessment report include?"**
   - Answer: A functional capacity assessment report includes: your diagnosis and medical history, assessment methodology used, detailed findings on each functional domain (mobility, self-care, communication, etc.), your current support needs, recommendations for supports and services, suggested NDIS funding areas, and goals for capacity building. The report is sent to the NDIA to inform your plan.

5. **"How long does a functional capacity assessment take?"**
   - Answer: A functional capacity assessment typically takes 2-4 hours depending on complexity. Some FCAs are completed in one session; others require 2-3 sessions. The assessor (usually an OT or physio) needs time to observe you doing various tasks and discuss your daily life thoroughly. Allow extra time if you fatigue easily.
